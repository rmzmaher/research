Waterflooding Optimisation Using Physics-Informed Reinforcement Learning

MDP formulation of injection-rate control, MARL with centralised critic, and physics-consistent reward design for NPV maximisation in secondary recovery.

1. Scope of the Research Area

Waterflooding is the dominant secondary recovery technique: water is injected through dedicated injector wells to maintain reservoir pressure and displace oil toward producers. Optimising injection rates across multiple wells is a high-dimensional, time-coupled, nonlinear control problem. Classical approaches — voidage replacement, streamline-based allocation, and adjoint optimisation — either ignore economic dynamics or require hard-coded simulator gradients that become intractable for compositional or thermal models.

Physics-Informed Reinforcement Learning (PI-RL) addresses this by embedding conservation-law residuals into the learning objective, reducing the number of simulator queries needed to converge, and producing policies that remain physically feasible. Key research questions include:

  • How should the multi-injector control problem be cast as a cooperative MARL problem with partial observability?
  • What state representation captures the reservoir's current recovery state without requiring full pressure–saturation field access?
  • How can physics residuals be incorporated into the reward signal without destabilising policy-gradient training?
  • How robust are learned policies under geological uncertainty (permeability realisations)?

2. Mathematical Foundations

Mass conservation for the water phase in porous media (neglecting capillary pressure and gravity for the fractional-flow formulation) gives the Buckley–Leverett equation [1]:

∂Sw/∂t + (qT/Aφ) dfw/dSw · ∂Sw/∂x = 0 (1)

where fw(Sw) = krw/μw / (krw/μw + kro/μo) is the fractional flow of water. In the full 3D multi-well setting, the governing pressure equation is:

∇·(λT k ∇p) = ∑w qw δ(x − xw) (2)

with total mobility λT = krw/μw + kro/μo. The NPV optimisation objective over discrete timesteps Δtk is:

max   J = ∑k=1NT (po Qo,k − cw Qw,k − cinj Qinj,k) Δtk / (1 + i)tk/365 (3)

subject to well constraints qinj,min ≤ qinj,w ≤ qinj,max and pBHP,min ≤ pBHP,w ≤ pBHP,max.

2.1 MARL CTDE formulation

Each injector i is an agent with local observation oi,t (cumulative injection, wellhead pressure, neighbouring producer WOR). A centralised critic receives the joint state–action tuple:

Qi(st, a1,t, …, aN,t; φi) → Vi(st; φi) (4)

Decentralised actors πθi(ai,t|oi,t) are updated via the policy gradient with advantage Ai = Qi − Vi [2].

4. Study Design Considerations

A valid waterflooding optimisation study should: (i) fix the reservoir model (synthetic or history-matched) and the geological uncertainty ensemble used for robustness testing; (ii) define the MDP explicitly — state, action granularity, episode length, and reward scaling; (iii) compare against at least one conventional baseline (reactive control or adjoint) using the same simulator and NPV parameters; and (iv) report wall-clock training time and number of simulator calls to allow reproducibility.

5. Validity Threats and Open Problems

  • Geological uncertainty: A policy optimised on the mean model may perform poorly across the uncertainty ensemble; robust RL (minimax or expected-NPV over realisations) remains computationally expensive.
  • Simulator fidelity: Coarse-grid training environments accelerate training but policies may not transfer to refined grids due to upscaling artefacts.
  • Non-stationarity: Well workover, drilling new wells, or changing surface constraints mid-episode invalidate the trained policy; online adaptation is required.
  • Physics constraint satisfaction: RL exploration may violate injection-pressure limits; safe RL with Lagrangian penalty or action masking is needed for field deployment.
  • Reward design: Sparse terminal NPV rewards cause slow convergence; shaped intermediate rewards must be carefully designed to avoid reward hacking.
