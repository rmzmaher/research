Physics-Informed Neural Networks for Subsurface Flow Systems

Research-area overview, governing formulations, algorithmic families, validation benchmarks, and open problems.

1. Scope of the Research Area

Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs), introduced by Raissi et al. [1], embed governing partial differential equations (PDEs) directly into the training objective of a neural surrogate. For subsurface energy systems the PINN paradigm sits at the intersection of scientific machine learning, reservoir engineering, and inverse problem theory. The research area addresses four interrelated questions:

  • How can physical conservation laws be encoded as differentiable soft or hard constraints?
  • Under what well-posedness and data regimes can PINNs recover unique solutions?
  • How do hybrid PINN–FV/FE solvers compare to classical discretisation for stiff, multiphase, heterogeneous problems?
  • How should uncertainty be quantified given approximation, optimisation, and generalisation error?

2. Mathematical Foundations

Let u(x,t) denote the unknown field over spatial domain Ω ⊂ ℝd and time interval [0, T]. A general PDE system takes the form:

∂u/∂t + 𝒥[u; λ] = 0,   x∈Ω, t∈(0,T] (1)

with boundary operator 𝒩[u]=g on ∂Ω and initial condition u(x,0)=u0. A PINN approximates the field by uθ(x,t) — a deep network parameterised by weights θ. The composite loss is:

ℒ(θ) = ωrℒPDE + ωbℒBC + ωiℒIC + ωdℒdata (2)
ℒPDE = (1/Nr) ∑k=1Nr |∂uθ/∂t + 𝒥[uθ; λ]|2 (3)

4. Study Design Considerations

A rigorous PINN study should declare (i) the well-posedness of the forward/inverse problem, (ii) the collocation strategy (uniform, Latin-hypercube, residual-based adaptive [11]), (iii) the training protocol (Adam → L-BFGS, curriculum on ωi), and (iv) the benchmark reference.

5. Validity Threats and Open Problems

  • Spectral bias: MLPs under-represent high-frequency components; remedies include Fourier features and SIREN activations [12].
  • Loss imbalance: Disparate residual scales cause one term to dominate; NTK analysis explains and mitigates this [4].
  • Optimisation landscape: PINN losses are highly non-convex; L-BFGS improves convergence but does not guarantee global minima.
  • Inverse identifiability: Recovering λ from sparse data requires sensitivity and uniqueness analysis.
  • Discrete conservation: PINNs minimise residuals pointwise, not in a finite-volume sense — mass-balance drift is a reported failure mode [10].
