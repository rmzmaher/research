Virtual Flow Metering for Artificial-Lift Wells

Multiphase rate estimation from readily available sensor measurements: first-principles, data-driven, and hybrid VFM model families with validation protocols.

1. Scope of the Research Area

Virtual Flow Metering (VFM) is the estimation of individual-phase production rates — oil, water, and gas — from sensor signals that are already available at the wellhead or downhole gauge without installing a dedicated multiphase flow meter (MPFM). The motivation is economic: a conventional MPFM costs US$200k–$2M in capital expenditure per well, requires calibration, and fails in high-GOR or emulsion regimes. VFM systems instead invert routinely measured operating variables (pressures, temperatures, motor current, choke opening) through either a physics-based or a learned model to infer the fluid rates [1].

The research area addresses four key questions:

  • Which sensor subset carries the most information about individual phase rates in a given lift configuration?
  • How do first-principles, empirical, and data-driven VFM models compare in accuracy, robustness to sensor drift, and interpretability?
  • How should VFM outputs be validated against periodic well tests, and what acceptance criteria are appropriate?
  • How can temporal models (LSTM, Conv1D) capture transient flow regimes that steady-state correlations miss?

2. Mathematical Foundations

The pressure-gradient equation for multiphase vertical flow in an ESP-lifted well integrates gravity, friction, and acceleration terms:

dp/dz = ρm g / gc + fm ρm vm2 / (2 d) + ρm vm dvm/dz (1)

where ρm = HLρL + (1−HL)ρg is the mixture density, HL the liquid holdup, vm the mixture velocity, and fm the Moody friction factor from a flow-regime-dependent closure [2]. The pump total dynamic head (TDH) balances available head against the well hydraulic system:

TDH = (pdisch − pintake) / (ρm g) + zpump (2)

Inverting Eq. (1)–(2) given measured pressures and pump curve yields liquid rate qL. Water cut WC = qw/qL requires an additional degree of freedom, typically estimated from density or dielectric sensors. In data-driven approaches, a mapping f: x → (qL, WC) is learned from labelled well-test measurements {(xi, qL,i, WCi)}.

2.1 Temporal model formulation (LSTM)

For time-series sensor inputs Xt−W:t ∈ ℝW×d of window width W, the LSTM hidden state evolves as:

ht, ct = LSTM(xt, ht−1, ct−1; θ) (3)

with cell-state update governed by forget, input, and output gates, capturing flow transients that steady-state mechanistic models miss during ramp-up or choke changes.

4. Study Design Considerations

A valid VFM study must declare: (i) the well-test reference protocol (frequency, duration, separator accuracy) against which VFM predictions are validated; (ii) the feature set and sensor availability under realistic field conditions including dropout; (iii) the temporal split for model evaluation — time-based hold-out prevents data leakage; and (iv) the acceptance criterion on mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) consistent with the operator's surveillance tolerance (typically <5% for liquid rate, <3 pp for WC).

5. Validity Threats and Open Problems

  • Sensor drift and calibration bias: Systematic offset in pressure or temperature sensors propagates directly into VFM predictions; periodic sensor health checks are a prerequisite.
  • Model extrapolation: Data-driven models trained at specific operating conditions (frequency range, WC range) degrade outside the training envelope without explicit bounds monitoring.
  • Well-test frequency: Sparse well-test labels (monthly or quarterly) limit the volume of labelled data available for supervised training.
  • Emulsion and gas interference: ESP performance curves are calibrated for clean single-phase liquid; high GOR or emulsion viscosity violates pump-curve assumptions in mechanistic models.
  • Deployment integration: Real-time VFM systems must handle missing data, out-of-order timestamps, and SCADA communication failures without producing spurious alarms.
