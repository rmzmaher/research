Reinforcement Learning for Petroleum Production Optimisation

Markov decision processes, policy-gradient methods, multi-agent coordination, and deployment in reservoir and artificial-lift control.

1. Scope of the Research Area

Reinforcement Learning (RL) addresses sequential decision-making problems in which an agent learns a policy that maximises cumulative reward through interaction with an environment — without requiring an explicit system model. In petroleum engineering the RL paradigm is applied across three nested problem classes:

  • Reservoir-scale production optimisation: injection-rate scheduling in waterflooding, steam flooding, and gas injection to maximise net present value (NPV).
  • Well-level artificial-lift control: ESP motor frequency tuning, gas-lift valve allocation, and sucker-rod pump stroke optimisation.
  • Field-level multi-well coordination: decentralised agents sharing global information through a centralised critic (CTDE paradigm).

The research area addresses four interrelated questions: (i) how to formulate the reservoir control problem as a Markov Decision Process (MDP); (ii) which algorithm family is most sample-efficient given simulator coupling costs; (iii) how to incorporate domain physics into the reward signal or transition dynamics; and (iv) how to transfer learned policies across heterogeneous reservoir types.

2. Mathematical Foundations

An MDP is defined by the tuple (𝒮, 𝒜, P, R, γ) where 𝒮 is the state space, 𝒜 the action space, P(s′|s,a) the transition kernel, R(s,a) the reward function, and γ ∈ [0,1) the discount factor [1]. The agent seeks policy πθ(a|s) that maximises the expected discounted return:

J(θ) = 𝔼π [∑t=0∞ γt R(st, at)] (1)

The action-value function Qπ(s,a) and state-value function Vπ(s) satisfy the Bellman equations:

Qπ(s,a) = R(s,a) + γ ∑s′ P(s′|s,a) Vπ(s′) (2)
Vπ(s) = ∑a π(a|s) Qπ(s,a) (3)

The advantage function A(s,a) = Q(s,a) − V(s) measures the relative benefit of action a over the average. The policy-gradient theorem [2] gives the gradient of the objective as:

∇θ J(θ) = 𝔼 [∇θ log πθ(a|s) · A(s,a)] (4)

For multi-agent systems with N agents, each agent i optimises its own cumulative reward subject to joint coordination constraints; the Centralised Training – Decentralised Execution (CTDE) paradigm maintains a centralised critic Qi(s, a1,…,aN) while deploying decentralised actors πθi(ai|oi) [3].

2.1 Reservoir NPV Reward Formulation

For injection control over timestep Δt, the reward signal integrates field-level economics:

Rt = (po qo − cw qw,prod − cinj qinj) Δt   /   (1 + i)t/365 (5)

where po is oil price, cw the water-handling cost, cinj the injection cost, and i the annual discount rate.

4. Study Design Considerations

A rigorous RL study in reservoir engineering must specify: (i) the MDP tuple explicitly including state representation, action granularity, reward scaling, and episode termination; (ii) the simulator coupling — numerical reservoir simulators (Eclipse, CMG STARS) act as the environment, so each episode requires a full forward simulation; (iii) the exploration strategy (ε-greedy, Gaussian noise, entropy bonus); and (iv) a baseline comparison against conventional optimisation (GA, adjoint gradients, reactive control).

5. Validity Threats and Open Problems

  • Simulator fidelity gap: Policies trained on coarse-grid or proxy models may not transfer to high-fidelity simulators; upscaling artefacts compound the sim-to-real gap.
  • Reward engineering: Choice of economic parameters (prices, discount rate) strongly influences the learned policy; sensitivity analysis is mandatory.
  • Partial observability: Downhole states (reservoir pressure, saturation) are not directly observed; the MDP becomes a POMDP requiring recurrent architectures or belief states.
  • Non-stationarity in MARL: As each agent updates its policy, the environment appears non-stationary to others, destabilising training without CTDE or parameter sharing.
  • Sample complexity: Reservoir simulations take minutes to hours; model-based RL or surrogate-assisted rollouts are required for acceptable wall-clock training times.
  • Constraint satisfaction: Hard operational constraints (max BHP, injection pressure) are violated during exploration; safe RL (Lagrangian or shielding) methods are an active research direction.
